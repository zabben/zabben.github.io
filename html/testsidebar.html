African grey

Size Diet Housing & environment Diseases Back to top

While most people think of the macaw when they hear the word parrot, the African grey parrot is actually the most commonly kept large parrot in homes around the world. Outside of our human homes, millions of African greys can be found in their natural habitat in equatorial African, but their numbers have been declining for many years.
African greys make great pets. They require a lot of your time and attention, but the rewards are aplenty. While sassy and sometimes moody, greys are fun and have strong personalities.

There are two kinds of African greys: Congo parrots (slightly bigger) and Timneh parrots. The Congo is roughly 15 inches long and weighs 500 grams, the Timneh 11 inches and weighs 300 grams. Like most large parrots, they are bound to be your companion for life: their lifespan ranges from 40 to 70 years.

Diet

While African greys in the wild can live off of seeds, nuts and fruits, our captive African greys can do no such thing. It is even dangerous to give your grey a handful of seeds, since they pick out two or three favorites and leave the rest be. The ones they do pick out are generally too fat and lack sufficient calcium and vitamin A. Greys are vulnerable to have a deficiency of these, so either make sure they have a supplement or get them to eat better. Feed your grey primarely pellets (for around 75% of its diet), and give it an extra piece of fruit, nut or veggie as an extra.

Housing & environment

It is recommended to get your grey as large a bird cage as possible, filled with many toys for them not to get bored during your absence. Make sure to alternate the toys you have hanging in the cage, so that your grey has some variety. A minimum cage size should be somewhere near 36 inch (91 cm) wide, 24 inch (60 cm) deep, and 40 inch (101 cm) high.

Diseases

A grey is susceptible to behavioral problems (like feather plucking), respitarory diseases, nasal blockages, vitamin A and D deficiency and hypocalcemia syndrome.
